# Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN)

## Convolutional Filters and Parameter Sharing
Convolutional filters, or kernels, are the foundational feature extraction mechanism in spatial neural architectures. Unlike fully connected layers where each neuron has a unique weight for every input pixel, convolutional layers utilize parameter sharing. A small matrix of weights, typically 3x3 or 5x5, slides across the entire input image, performing an element-wise multiplication and sum at each spatial location. Because the same filter weights are applied universally across the image, the network achieves translation invariance—it can detect a specific feature, like a vertical edge or a texture, regardless of where it appears in the visual field. This sliding window operation drastically reduces the total number of trainable parameters compared to dense layers, preventing massive overfitting while preserving the highly structured spatial relationships inherent in two-dimensional visual data.

## Pooling Layers and Downsampling
Pooling layers are spatial downsampling operations strategically placed between convolutional layers to reduce the dimensionality of feature maps. The most prevalent technique is Max Pooling, which slides a window—often 2x2 with a stride of 2—across the feature map and outputs only the maximum activation value within that window. This operation serves three critical functions. First, it aggressively reduces the computational load and memory footprint for subsequent layers. Second, it limits the number of parameters, acting as a natural regularization technique to combat overfitting. Finally, it provides minor spatial invariance; exact pixel locations become less important than the general presence of a feature in a specific region. By discarding exact spatial coordinates while retaining the most prominent extracted features, pooling allows the architecture to build an increasingly abstract, high-level representation of the input.

## The Receptive Field
The receptive field refers to the specific spatial region of the original input image that influences a particular neuron's activation value. In the first convolutional layer, a neuron's receptive field is exactly equal to the kernel size, such as a 3x3 pixel area. However, as layers are stacked deeper into the network, the effective receptive field grows exponentially. A neuron in the third or fourth layer aggregates information from neurons in the preceding layer, which themselves aggregated information from previous layers. This hierarchical expansion allows deep neurons to "see" a massive portion of the original image, enabling them to recognize complex, global structures like a face or a car, while early neurons are restricted to localized patterns like simple diagonal lines or color gradients.
